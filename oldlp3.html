Creative Piece: Tragic Hero or Coward?

By now, we’ve all read All My Sons, by Arthur Miller. The story was intriguing, and I must admit… I finished it all in one sitting. One thought that’s been endlessly drifting around my head, space oddity style, is this: How does one classify the main character, Joe Keller? I’ve done my own research, and I found a lot of english teachers, or current literature enthusiasts much like you and I, that believed he was a tragic hero, defined by Aristotle as “ a literary character who makes a judgment error that inevitably leads to his/her own destruction”, and this particular opinion caught my attention. I strongly disagree, and in this article, I want to show you, the audience, why I think Joe Keller is the furthest thing from a hero that a character can be

Firstly, I’m gonna put a little “Debate Club” twist on this, and look at previously existing arguments that say he’s a tragic hero, and debunk them. By the definition of a tragic hero, a character must make a fatal mistake, or a judgement error, that leads to their demise. Every argument I’ve read has cited the fact that Joe Keller made one single mistake, or his flaw, by allowing the shipment of dysfunctional goods, and that he put his family before his country, which eventually led to his demise. I think that there has to be a clear distinction between a flaw, or a lapse in judgement, and complete and utter evil. Personally, I find it difficult to believe that condemning dozens to die, including his own son, doesn’t particularly fall under the “tragic hero” type of mistake.

Here’s my point: Joe Keller’s “flaw” was that he was a capitalist, valuing the life of his family over others. Not only that, but how he was viewed in the community as well. Not only did he sentence many pilots to a gruesome death, but he also lied, therefore leading his business partner to jail. In the context of this story, these actions simply cannot make him a hero. Besides, if Joe Keller was to kill himself either way, he did it only after discovering that it was his son that died as an indirect result of his actions, not when he lied to the police, or killed many pilots. And again, the reason he commit suicide was because of how he thought his family would view him. Instead of the contemporary tragic hero, such as Danaerys Targaryen, or Severus Snape, ranging all the way back to Hamlet, or Romeo, Joe Keller’s character and background simply doesn’t follow the template of a tragic hero. Instead, his sick, self serving actions lead him to extreme guilt, only when a family member dies at his hands, which goes to show that his psychopath-like actions never served as indications towards a tragic hero, but rather the antagonist of All My Sons.
